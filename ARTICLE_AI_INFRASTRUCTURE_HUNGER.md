# The $690 Billion "Addiction" Nobody Talks About

## Why the Real AI Trade Isn't Software. It's Copper, Uranium, and Cold Air.

---

Everyone is talking about AI models. Nobody is talking about what keeps them alive.

In 2026, Amazon, Google, Meta, and Microsoft will spend a combined $690 billion on infrastructure. That is more than Sweden's entire GDP. Three quarters of it - roughly $450 billion - goes directly into servers, GPUs, and data centers.

Not software. Hardware. Physical things that need electricity, cooling, and raw materials.

Amazon alone is spending $200 billion this year. Their free cash flow is going negative - Morgan Stanley projects a $17 billion deficit, Bank of America says $28 billion. Alphabet's free cash flow is projected to drop 90% - from $73.3 billion to $8.2 billion. Capital intensity has reached historically unthinkable levels: 45-57% of revenue.

These companies are burning through cash faster than they can generate it. And they are accelerating, not slowing down.

This article is about the part of AI that nobody on Crypto Twitter discusses: the physical infrastructure underneath the code. Where the real bottlenecks are. And where the actual investment opportunities hide.

---

## TL;DR - The Cheat Sheet

1. AI is no longer a software story. It is an infrastructure and energy story.
2. Data centers will consume 945-1,050 TWh by 2030 - growing 15% per year, 4x faster than all other sectors combined.
3. The bottleneck is copper: global demand rises from 28 MMt (2025) to 42 MMt (2040). Supply peaks at 33 MMt in 2030. That is a 10 million metric ton gap.
4. The hyperscalers are supply-constrained, not demand-constrained. Google's cloud backlog surged 55% to $240 billion.
5. The real trade: infrastructure suppliers and raw materials, not the AI companies themselves.

Alphabet's CEO Sundar Pichai said the scale is "significant enough to cause concern internally" - but then pointed to a cloud backlog that surged 55% sequentially to over $240 billion.

Translation: the market wants more AI than physically exists.

## Part 2: The Energy Problem Nobody Can Solve Fast Enough

Data centers consumed about 415 TWh globally in 2024. That was roughly 1.5% of global electricity.

By 2030, the IEA projects data center consumption reaching 945 TWh in the base case - and potentially 1,300 TWh by 2035. Data center electricity consumption is growing at 15% per year, more than four times faster than the growth of all other sectors combined.

In the US alone, data centers now consume 4% of all electricity. By 2030, that could reach 14%.

But here is the part most people miss: cooling accounts for 7% to 40% of a data center's total energy consumption, depending on the facility. For less efficient enterprise data centers, cooling eats almost half the power bill.

And cooling does not just consume electricity - it consumes water. In 2023, US data centers consumed 17 billion gallons of water. By 2028, hyperscale data centers alone will consume between 16 and 33 billion gallons annually.

The formula is simple but brutal:

**More GPUs = More heat = More cooling = More water + More electricity = More infrastructure = More CapEx**

Each layer multiplies the cost of the next. This is not a linear scaling problem. It is exponential.

Global electricity demand is shifting. According to S&P Global's data (Figure 7), electricity demand will grow +47% by 2040, overtaking oil as the dominant energy source. AI is a major driver of this shift.

## Part 3: The Copper Crisis

This is the section that matters most. And it is the one nobody on CT is talking about.

### The Demand Explosion

S&P Global released a landmark study in January 2026: "Copper in the Age of AI." The numbers are devastating.

Global copper demand will rise from 28 million metric tons in 2025 to 42 million metric tons by 2040. A 50% increase. Here is what is driving the additional 14 MMt:

| Demand Vector | Share of Growth | MMt Added |
|--------------|----------------|-----------|
| Energy transition & electrification | 50% | ~7 MMt |
| Core economic growth | 36% | ~5 MMt |
| AI & data centers | 10% | ~1.4 MMt |
| Defense | 4% | ~0.6 MMt |
| **Total additional** | **100%** | **+14 MMt** |

At first glance, AI is "only" 10% of the growth. But that 10% is the most concentrated, fastest-growing, and hardest to substitute. AI data center copper demand will account for 58% of total data center copper demand by 2030. And data centers are just one piece - the grid infrastructure they require (substations, transformers, transmission lines) multiplies the copper demand several times over.

### The Supply Problem

Here is the real problem: supply cannot keep up.

Primary mined copper supply is projected to peak at 27 million metric tons in 2030, then decline to 22 million by 2040. Even with recycled copper (secondary supply reaching 10 MMt), total supply maxes out at around 32 million metric tons. Against 42 MMt of demand.

That leaves a 10 million metric ton gap by 2040.

S&P Global's exact words: "Without meaningful expansion, the primary mined supply is set to peak in 2030." They call this a "systemic risk for global industries, technological advancement, and economic growth."

You cannot build data centers without copper. You cannot mine copper fast enough. And you cannot substitute it - no other metal has copper's combination of conductivity, ductility, and corrosion resistance.

### Where the Copper Goes

Copper is in every layer of a data center:
- **Power delivery:** cables, bus bars, switchgear, transformers
- **Cooling systems:** copper tubing, heat exchangers
- **IT infrastructure:** circuit boards, connectors, wiring
- **Grid connection:** substations, transmission lines, distribution networks

A single hyperscale data center uses 20,000-40,000 tonnes of copper. With hundreds being built simultaneously, the bottleneck is real and getting worse.

### The Regional Picture

China and APAC dominate copper demand growth. According to Figure 3 from the S&P report:

| Region | 2025-2040 Growth (MMt) | Share |
|--------|----------------------|-------|
| China | +4.8 | 34% |
| APAC (ex. China) | +3.5 | 25% |
| Europe | +1.9 | 14% |
| USA | +1.4 | 10% |
| Middle East | +0.5 | 4% |
| Others | +1.8 | 13% |

China and APAC combined represent ~60% of all copper demand growth. This is not just an American problem - it is a global competition for a finite resource.

### Core Economic Demand

Even without AI, copper demand from traditional sectors is growing at 2% CAGR through 2040 (Figure 4):
- Construction: 8 MMt (2025) to 10 MMt (2040)
- Machinery: 7 MMt to 9 MMt
- Cooling & appliances: growing at 3.4% CAGR, driven by AC demand in emerging markets

Air conditioning alone is the largest single driver of copper demand in appliances - representing 57-61% of the cooling/appliances category. As global temperatures rise, AC demand rises with them, creating yet another layer of copper demand competing with data centers.

## Part 4: Uranium, Nuclear, and the Power Bottleneck

Nuclear power supplied roughly 20% of US data center electricity in 2024. The appeal is obvious: stable baseload power with predictable costs and zero carbon emissions.

Key developments:
- Microsoft signed a deal to restart Three Mile Island Unit 1 - 837 MW of carbon-free power, enough for a campus running hundreds of thousands of GPUs
- Google contracted Kairos Power for small modular reactors (SMRs) that can deploy in 3-5 years instead of 10-15
- 63% of global investors now believe AI-related consumption will become a "material factor" in nuclear planning within the next decade

But nuclear has a critical constraint: you cannot build a reactor in a year. Traditional plants take 10-15 years. Even SMRs need 3-5 years. The buildout timeline does not match AI's growth curve.

The workaround: long-term contracts with existing nuclear plants. But even then, you need:
1. **Grid connection confirmation** - officially fixed capacity at the connection point with an energization date
2. **Network studies completion** - technical studies on the grid side, signed transmission agreements
3. **Peak hour flexibility** - nuclear provides flat baseload, but AI inference creates demand spikes that baseload cannot handle alone

Without all three, the cheap base rate does not deliver the expected economics. Queue delays can add years and millions in costs.

Data center installed capacity is projected to reach 550 GW by 2040 - more than 5x what it was in 2022. That power has to come from somewhere.

## Part 5: The Cooling Arms Race

Air cooling dominated for decades. But GPU density is killing it. Modern AI chips generate so much heat per square meter that air simply cannot remove it fast enough.

The shift to liquid cooling is inevitable:
- Higher efficiency (removes more heat per watt)
- Allows denser rack configurations (more GPUs per square meter)
- Reduces total data center footprint

Geography becomes a competitive advantage. Data centers in Norway, Finland, and Sweden use free cooling from cold outside air most of the year, dramatically reducing operating costs. Microsoft tested underwater data center modules for two years starting in 2018.

But there are trade-offs: cold climate locations often lack proximity to users (latency), grid capacity, and water access. Europe already has regulatory restrictions on water usage for data centers.

The most likely near-term solution: hybrid cooling systems (air + liquid) in existing locations, with fully liquid-cooled facilities for new GPU-dense deployments.

The cooling market is where nobody is looking - and where margins are highest.

## Part 6: The Counterintuitive Trade

Here is what most people think:

"AI is booming. Buy NVDA."

Here is what the data actually says:

The hyperscalers are spending $690 billion. Nvidia captures maybe $100-150 billion of that in GPU sales. Where does the other $500+ billion go?

- **Power infrastructure** (utilities, grid upgrades, transformers)
- **Raw materials** (copper miners, aluminum producers, uranium miners)
- **Cooling systems** (liquid cooling companies, HVAC manufacturers)
- **Construction** (data center builders, electrical contractors)
- **Real estate** (land near cheap power and cold climates)

The GPU is 15-20% of total data center cost. The other 80% is "boring" infrastructure that nobody tweets about.

This is the classic "sell shovels during a gold rush" setup. But this time the shovels are copper cables, nuclear fuel rods, and liquid cooling systems.

Here is the counterintuitive part: the companies with the WORST financial outlook right now are the hyperscalers themselves. Amazon is burning $17-28 billion in negative FCF. Alphabet's cash generation collapsed 90%. They are the ones spending the money.

The companies RECEIVING that money - copper miners, utilities, cooling manufacturers, construction firms - are seeing demand they have never experienced before. And unlike AI software, their products cannot be disrupted by the next model release.

## Part 7: The Prediction Market Angle

Polymarket currently hosts 361 active AI markets. But almost all of them are about software milestones - "Will GPT-5 launch before Q3?" or "AGI before 2027?"

What Polymarket does NOT have markets on:
- Will copper prices exceed $12,000/tonne in 2026?
- Will any hyperscaler delay a data center due to power constraints?
- Will the US approve a new nuclear reactor specifically for AI?
- Will liquid cooling become standard in >50% of new data centers by 2027?
- Will total data center electricity consumption exceed 600 TWh in 2026?

The infrastructure story is completely unpriced in prediction markets. When these markets eventually appear, the edge will belong to people who understood the physical layer of AI before the crowd.

## Part 8: The Bigger Picture

If you zoom out far enough, you see something remarkable in the S&P Global energy data (Figure 7):

Electricity demand will grow 47% by 2040, overtaking oil as the world's dominant energy carrier. AI is not the only driver - EVs, heat pumps, and industrial electrification all contribute - but AI is the fastest-growing and most capital-intensive component.

The AI boom is not a software revolution. It is an industrial revolution. And industrial revolutions are won by the companies that supply the raw materials.

Think about it this way:

The internet boom of the 2000s made a few software companies rich. But the real, steady money went to the companies laying fiber optic cables, building cell towers, and manufacturing routers. Most of those companies are still profitable today. Most of the dot-com software companies are dead.

History does not repeat, but it rhymes.

The question is not whether AI will transform the world. It will.

The question is whether the physical infrastructure can keep up. Right now, it cannot. And every dollar of that gap is an opportunity.
