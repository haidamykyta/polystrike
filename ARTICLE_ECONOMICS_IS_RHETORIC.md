# Economics Is Rhetoric. Prediction Markets Proved It.

## How Words Move Billions - and Why the Smartest Traders Bet on Stories, Not Data.

---

Everyone thinks markets run on numbers. They do not. Markets run on stories.

In April 2025, one speech wiped $10 trillion from global markets in two days. Not a recession. Not a financial crisis. A speech. Trump's "Liberation Day" tariff announcement triggered the worst two-day loss in US stock market history.

Then, seven days later, a tweet paused the tariffs. The S&P 500 posted one of its largest single-day rallies in decades.

$10 trillion destroyed by words. Billions restored by words. The actual economic impact? Somewhere in between. Penn Wharton estimated a 6% long-run GDP reduction. Significant, but nowhere near the market reaction in either direction.

This is not a bug. This is how markets actually work. And an economist named Deirdre McCloskey explained why back in 1983.

4. George Soros said the same thing differently: "Financial markets always provide a distorted view of reality."

## Part 1: The Professor Who Called It

In 1983, Deirdre McCloskey, an economist at the University of Chicago, published a paper that made her colleagues uncomfortable. The title was simple: "The Rhetoric of Economics."

Her argument: economics is not a hard science that discovers objective truths. It is a social practice built on persuasion. When an economist presents a model, they are not revealing facts like a physicist measuring gravity. They are constructing a narrative. Choosing which variables to highlight. Deciding which risks to call "key" and which to bury in footnotes.

McCloskey wrote: "Economists use stories as geologists do, and metaphors as physicists do." She argued that even the most mathematical moments in economics are "properly dominated by comparison and narration - that is to say, human persuasion."

Or more bluntly: "Economics is words all the way down."

At the time, most economists dismissed her. Economics was supposed to be rigorous. Scientific. Data-driven. The idea that it was fundamentally a form of storytelling felt like an insult.

Forty years later, prediction markets proved she was right.

## Part 2: The Same Number, Two Opposite Trades

Here is a simple test of McCloskey's thesis.

When the US Bureau of Labor Statistics releases a CPI report, the number is the number. It is not subjective. It is measured, calculated, published. Everyone sees the same figure at the same time.

So why do different analysts reach opposite conclusions from the same report?

In April 2025, headline CPI rose 0.3% and core CPI came in at 0.2% - slightly below expectations. Here is how the same data was interpreted:

The bears said: "Goods prices were higher. This is exactly what tariff-driven inflation looks like. Expect more."

The bulls said: "Service sector prices, which account for almost 80% of CPI, remained stable. Tariffs are not flowing through to the real economy."

Same report. Same numbers. Opposite trades.

This is what McCloskey meant. The data does not speak for itself. Someone always has to interpret it, frame it, present it. And that presentation - the choice of which chart to show first, which line to bold, which risk to emphasize - is rhetoric.

As McCloskey put it: "Content arises from the metaphors and stories with which economists make their world, and content is not separable from the style and arrangement."

You have seen this yourself. A strong Non-Farm Payrolls number can mean "the economy is healthy" (bullish) or "the Fed will not cut rates" (bearish). The same data point supports both narratives. The market follows whichever story is more convincing that week.

## Part 3: When Words Are Worth $10 Trillion

If economics is rhetoric, then the people with the best rhetoric should move markets the most. And they do.

Fed Chair Jerome Powell's words routinely move markets more than the actual data he is commenting on. In early 2025, Powell said tariff impacts would be "significantly larger than expected." That statement moved bond yields and equities more than the April CPI print itself - a report that showed tariffs had not yet been "meaningfully reflected in the data."

The market did not trade the number. It traded the story Powell told about the number.

This is not new. But the scale has changed dramatically.

In 2022, during the inflation crisis, 48% of S&P 500 trading days had moves greater than 1%. Markets were hypersensitive to every data release. By 2024, that number dropped to normal levels - average daily volatility fell to 0.91%.

Then came 2025. Volatility jumped back to 1.18%. But not because of inflation data. Because of tariff rhetoric. Speeches, tweets, press conferences. Words, not numbers, drove the largest market swings in years.

George Soros described this phenomenon decades ago with his theory of reflexivity: "Financial markets, far from accurately reflecting all the available knowledge, always provide a distorted view of reality."

Soros went further: markets do not merely discount the future - they help shape it. When enough participants believe a narrative, their actions make it real. A bank run is not caused by insolvency. It is caused by the story that the bank might be insolvent. The narrative creates the reality it describes.

McCloskey and Soros are saying the same thing from different angles. The economist says: "We are all storytellers." The trader says: "The story IS the market."

## Part 4: Prediction Markets - The Ultimate Rhetoric Machine

If you want to see McCloskey's thesis in its purest form, look at prediction markets.

Prediction markets are supposed to be the most efficient information aggregation mechanism ever invented. Polymarket CEO Shayne Coplan has called his platform "the most accurate thing we have as mankind right now."

But here is what actually happens on Polymarket:

**The French Whale (October 2024)**

A single trader from France - later identified as "Theo" - wagered $80 million across four accounts that Trump would win the 2024 election. His bets pushed Trump's odds to 70-30 while polls showed a much closer race.

His thesis was not based on polling data. It was based on a narrative: the "shy Trump voter" effect. He commissioned his own informal polling - asking people about their social circles rather than who they would personally vote for.

One person's narrative conviction moved an entire prediction market. Academic studies later confirmed it: "Polymarket attracts large, aggressive, and risk-seeking speculators, and because the platform does not cap positions, one player can move entire markets, producing prices that reflect individual beliefs rather than collective wisdom."

The wisdom of the crowd became the conviction of one storyteller with deep pockets.

**Biden Debate Withdrawal (June-July 2024)**

After Biden's June 27 debate performance, Polymarket odds of him dropping out spiked from 20% to 70% within days. Then surged to 80% after a New York Times report and Obama expressing concerns.

No official announcement had been made. No data changed. The rhetoric shifted - "Biden cannot do this" became the dominant narrative - and $100 million in trading volume followed the story.

**The Assassination Asymmetry**

Trump survived two assassination attempts in 2024. The first one (July) sent his Polymarket odds jumping from 60% to 70% - a massive move. The second one (September) barely moved the needle.

Same category of event. Same candidate. Wildly different market reactions. Why? Because the narrative context changed. In July, Trump was running against Biden and the "sympathy vote" story was powerful. By September, Harris had replaced Biden and the assassination narrative had lost its rhetorical force.

The event was the same. The story around it was different. And it was the story, not the event, that determined the price.

## Part 5: The Insider's Edge Is a Narrative Edge

Here is where it gets uncomfortable.

In January 2026, an anonymous trader created a Polymarket account one week before a US raid captured Venezuelan dictator Maduro. The trader became the largest holder of "yes" contracts on Maduro leaving office - at 5.5% odds - and made $400,000 in profit.

In February 2026, an account called "Magamyman" made $553,000 betting on events related to Iran's Supreme Leader Khamenei just before an Israeli strike killed him.

These are extreme examples. But they illustrate something fundamental about how information and narrative interact in markets.

What did these traders have? Not better models. Not better data analysis. They had the narrative before everyone else. They knew which story was about to become true.

Senator Chris Murphy put it starkly: "People around Trump are profiting off war and death." Congressman Ritchie Torres introduced insider trading legislation for prediction markets.

But here is the deeper point: even without insider information, every trade is a bet on a narrative. When you buy YES on "Fed rate cut before July," you are not pricing a mathematical probability. You are betting that the story of economic weakness will be more persuasive than the story of persistent inflation. You are betting on rhetoric.

## Part 6: Keynes Knew. Soros Knew. Now You Know.

McCloskey was not the first to notice this. She was just the first to say it so directly.

John Maynard Keynes described markets as a "beauty contest" in 1936. The game is not to pick the most beautiful face. It is to pick the face you think everyone else will pick. "Anticipating what average opinion expects the average opinion to be."

That is pure rhetoric. You are not analyzing value. You are analyzing which story will be most persuasive to the most people.

Robert Shiller, the Nobel laureate, built on this with his concept of "narrative economics" in 2019: "Regardless of whether they are true or false, narratives can drive the economy by influencing our decisions about how and where to invest, and how much to spend and save."

True or false. Shiller is saying that accuracy is not the point. Persuasiveness is.

Benjamin Graham gave us the most famous framing: "In the short run, the market is a voting machine, but in the long run, it is a weighing machine."

The voting machine is rhetoric. The weighing machine is data. And for traders operating on anything shorter than a decade, you are trading the voting machine. You are trading rhetoric.

Even Nassim Taleb, the ultimate skeptic, implied the same thing from the opposite direction: "If an investment proposal reads like a compelling story, odds are it is not going to work." The better the narrative, the more suspicious you should be.

## Part 7: The Prediction Market Paradox

Here is the counterintuitive part.

An academic study of the Iowa Electronic Markets found that traders were systematically biased - "they bought more contracts of the candidate they supported than they sold." They were not rational actors pricing objective probabilities. They were narrative-driven participants betting on stories they wanted to be true.

And yet the markets still predicted outcomes more accurately than polls.

How? Because prediction markets do not require all traders to behave rationally. They require enough traders with enough capital to eventually push prices toward reality. The rhetoric cancels out - eventually.

But "eventually" can be a long time. And in the meantime, the gap between the narrative price and the reality price is where traders make money.

A 2025 academic paper called this "prediction laundering": the "wisdom" produced by prediction markets is "not a reflection of universal human speculation, but a subset of possibilities that have been pre-formatted for blockchain settlement." Participant inputs - "ranging from data-driven analysis to emotional bias" - get "flattened into a singular numerical percentage."

In other words: prediction markets take stories and convert them into numbers. The numbers look objective. But they are still stories underneath.

## Part 8: What This Means for Your Trades

I do not think McCloskey is saying economics is fake. I do not think markets are pure manipulation.

What she is saying - and what Soros, Keynes, Shiller, and prediction markets all confirm - is that the story around the data matters at least as much as the data itself. Sometimes more.

For traders, this means:

**Stop asking "what does the data say?" Start asking "what story is the market telling about this data?"**

The same unemployment report is bullish in one narrative environment and bearish in another. The number did not change. The story did.

**Watch the storytellers, not just the statistics.** Fed speakers, politicians, influential analysts - these are the people who frame how data is received. Powell saying "significantly larger than expected" was worth more than the CPI print itself.

**In prediction markets, follow the narrative shifts.** When Polymarket odds move sharply on no new information, that is a story changing. The French Whale did not have better data than anyone else. He had a stronger conviction in a particular narrative.

**Be honest about your own rhetoric.** We all do this. When you are long, you emphasize the bullish data and dismiss the bearish signals. When you are short, you do the opposite. You are not analyzing objectively. You are building a case. Arguing a position. Doing rhetoric.

As McCloskey would say: the first person you need to stop persuading is yourself.

---

The next time you see a weak market reaction to strong data, do not be surprised. The market is just waiting for someone to step up to the microphone and tell it what those numbers really mean.

Because in the end, economics is not about what the numbers say. It is about who tells the most convincing story about them.
